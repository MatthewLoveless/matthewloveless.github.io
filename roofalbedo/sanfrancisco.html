Warm Roofs, Hot Cities Mapping Roof Albedo in San Francisco, California

  • This map displays roof albedo (solar reflectance) estimates from Berkeley Lab for San Francisco.

    Albedo is the fraction of sunlight that is reflected from a surface. It is measured on a scale of 0 to 1, where higher numbers indicate greater reflectance.

    Reflective roofs are more energy efficient and cooler inside and out. In San Francisco, the average roof albedo is about 0.18.
  • + A sample cool roof

    This roof has a high albedo and reflects about 60% of the energy from sunlight. On a sunny day, a roof that strongly reflects sunlight can stay much cooler than a roof that absorbs sunlight. Click on the green marker to view this building in Google Maps.

  • + A sample warm roof

    This building has a dark roof that absorbs sunlight and warms both the building and the surrounding air. The building has an albedo of about 0.15, meaning that about 85% of the solar energy is absorbed. Click on the yellow marker to view this building in Google Maps.

With funding from the California Air Resources Board, Berkeley Lab's Heat Island Group created this map using data acquired via an airplane-mounted sensor during summer 2009. The sensor measured light-intensity in four spectral bands at 1-meter spatial resolution, and was originally acquired for the National Agriculture Imagery Program.

After various corrections were made (e.g. accounting for light scattering in the atmosphere), individual rooftop pixels were isolated, and the albedo of each roof was estimated. The results were calibrated and “ground-truthed” using laboratory measurements of hundreds of roofing samples and rooftop measurements of albedo using an instrument called a pyranometer. Streets and parking lots were excluded from the analysis.

Note: Rooftop equipment, such as solar panels or HVAC systems, was not distinguished from the roofing material and was included in the albedo estimate for each rooftop. Also note that there is associated uncertainty in the reported albedo for each rooftop, especially for high albedo roofs. For more details on methods, uncertainties, and further analyses and implications, see the CARB report.

At noon on a clear summer day in the United States, a flat roof receives about 1000 watts of sunlight per square meter. Traditional dark roofs strongly absorb sunlight and:

  • Heat up the building.
  • Increase AC energy use, cost, and associated climate warming greenhouse gas emissions
  • Heat the air, worsening the urban heat island effect.
